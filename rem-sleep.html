What Happens During REM Sleep?

Understanding one of the most important stages of your sleep cycle.

REM stands for Rapid Eye Movement — a phase of sleep where your brain becomes more active and dreaming occurs. It’s also a time when your body temporarily loses muscle tone, which is important for people with sleep apnea to understand.

Why REM Sleep Is Important

  • Memory: Helps with learning and emotional regulation
  • Dreaming: Most vivid dreams happen in REM
  • Brain health: Thought to aid in mental clarity and cognitive function

What Makes REM Different?

  • Heart rate and breathing become less regular
  • Your brain is active but your muscles are paralyzed — which prevents you from acting out dreams
  • Sleep apnea is often worse during REM due to reduced airway muscle tone

Bottom Line

REM sleep is essential for mental and physical health — and it's often disrupted in people with sleep apnea. Effective treatment can help restore a healthy sleep cycle and more consistent REM sleep.
