Understanding Sleep-Disordered Breathing

Learn about how sleep-disordered breathing affects your health and what symptoms to watch for.

Types of Sleep-Disordered Breathing

Primary Snoring: Noisy breathing during sleep caused by vibration of tissues in the throat (such as the soft palate or uvula). It does not involve pauses in breathing or drops in oxygen, but may still disrupt sleep and indicate airflow restriction.

Snoring Facts:
  • Often worsens with age and weight gain
  • More common in men and those with nasal congestion
  • Can be an early warning sign of OSA

Upper Airway Resistance Syndrome (UARS): A condition where the airway narrows slightly during sleep, causing increased effort to breathe. Though oxygen levels typically remain stable, this leads to repeated arousals (micro-awakenings), resulting in unrefreshing sleep and daytime fatigue.

Causes:
  • Small jaw or narrow airway anatomy
  • Increased nasal resistance or turbinate hypertrophyn
  • More common in younger, thinner individuals

Obstructive Sleep Apnea (OSA): A more serious condition where the airway becomes partially or completely blocked repeatedly during sleep. These episodes can last 10 seconds or longer, leading to reduced oxygen levels, poor sleep quality, and long-term health risks. OSA severity is measured by the Apnea-Hypopnea Index (AHI) on a sleep study.

Did you know?
  • OSA affects over 20 million Americans, but many are undiagnosed
  • Risk factors include obesity, neck circumference >17 inches in men
  • Untreated OSA increases the risk of hypertension and stroke

Common Symptoms

  • Loud, chronic snoring
  • Excessive daytime fatigue
  • Morning headaches
  • Dry mouth or sore throat upon waking
  • Waking up gasping or choking
  • Trouble staying asleep or frequent awakenings
  • Brain fog or memory and focus issues
  • Mood changes, such as irritability or depression
  • Decreased libido
  • Difficulty losing weight

Note: Many people with SDB don’t realize they’re waking up throughout the night.

Why It Matters

Left untreated, sleep-disordered breathing can:

  • Disrupt your body’s natural sleep cycles
  • Lead to serious health issues:
    • High blood pressure
    • Heart attack or heart failure
    • Stroke
    • Type 2 diabetes
    • Atrial fibrillation (irregular heartbeat)
  • Increase your risk of drowsy driving or workplace accidents
  • Decrease overall quality of life

The good news: Identifying and treating SDB can lead to better sleep, more energy, improved mood, and long-term health benefits.
