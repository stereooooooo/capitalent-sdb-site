Do You Really Need a Sleep Study?

What sleep studies measure, who should get one, and what results mean.

If you snore, feel tired during the day, or have been told you stop breathing while asleep, your doctor may recommend a sleep study. But what does it involve — and is it really necessary?

What a Sleep Study Measures

  • Breathing: Airflow, respiratory effort, and oxygen levels
  • Brain waves: To track sleep stages (used in lab studies)
  • Heart rate: To watch for abnormal rhythms
  • Movement: To monitor leg activity or restlessness

Types of Sleep Studies

  • Home Sleep Apnea Test (HSAT): Convenient, done in your own bed, and used to diagnose obstructive sleep apnea
  • Polysomnography (PSG): Performed in a sleep lab, provides more detailed data — often used for complex cases

Why It Matters

Sleep apnea can lead to serious health problems if left untreated. A sleep study gives your care team objective data to guide treatment — whether it's CPAP, an oral appliance, or something else.

Bottom Line

If you’ve been advised to get a sleep study, it’s for a good reason. It’s the best way to confirm whether you have sleep apnea and how severe it is — which helps us help you breathe and sleep better.
