Sleep and Allergies

Understanding how allergies affect your sleep and what you can do about it.

Many people with allergies notice that their symptoms get worse at night. Sneezing, congestion, and post-nasal drip can all make it harder to fall asleep or stay asleep. But what you might not realize is that allergies can also contribute directly to sleep-disordered breathing, including snoring and obstructive sleep apnea (OSA).

How Allergies Disrupt Sleep

When you're exposed to allergens like dust mites, pollen, pet dander, or mold, your immune system reacts by releasing histamines and other inflammatory chemicals. These responses cause:

  • Nasal congestion: Swelling of the nasal lining and increased mucus production block airflow through your nose.
  • Mouth breathing: When your nose is congested, you're more likely to breathe through your mouth during sleep, which can dry out your airway and increase snoring.
  • Airway collapse: Increased resistance in the nasal passages can contribute to collapsibility of the upper airway, especially during REM sleep when muscle tone is reduced.
  • Fragmented sleep: Frequent awakenings from nasal obstruction or difficulty breathing lead to poor sleep quality and daytime fatigue.

Even mild or seasonal allergies can cause enough upper airway inflammation to trigger or worsen existing sleep-disordered breathing.

Treatment Options

At Capital ENT & Sinus Center, we specialize in treating both allergies and sleep-disordered breathing. We offer:

  • Allergy testing to identify specific triggers
  • Allergy immunotherapy (allergy shots or drops) to reduce sensitivity over time
  • Medical therapy with nasal sprays, antihistamines, or decongestants
  • Nasal and sinus surgery if structural issues are contributing to obstruction

By treating allergies directly, we can often improve sleep quality, reduce snoring, and in some cases lower the severity of obstructive sleep apnea.

Bottom Line

If you're struggling with both allergies and poor sleep, the two may be more connected than you think. Managing allergies can make a big difference in how you breathe—and how well you sleep—at night.
