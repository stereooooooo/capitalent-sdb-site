When Snoring Is a Sign of Something More

Snoring is common — but it’s not always harmless.

Snoring happens when air flows past relaxed tissues in the throat, causing vibration. While it can be harmless in some people, loud and frequent snoring may be a warning sign of obstructive sleep apnea (OSA).

How to Tell the Difference

  • Primary snoring: Smooth, steady snoring without major pauses or gasping
  • Sleep apnea: Snoring interrupted by breathing pauses, gasping, or choking
  • Partner observations: Often it’s the bed partner who notices signs of apnea

Why It Matters

  • Sleep apnea increases the risk of heart disease, high blood pressure, and daytime fatigue
  • Snoring alone can still disrupt your bed partner’s sleep and affect your relationship
  • Sleep studies can distinguish between primary snoring and apnea

Bottom Line

If you snore regularly or wake up tired, don’t ignore it. It may be a simple noise — or a signal of something deeper that deserves attention.
